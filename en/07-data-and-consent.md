# 07. The forgotten layer: data and consent

[← Index](../README.md) · [← 06 What could flip the table](06-what-could-flip-the-table.md)

> *This chapter extends the [four-layer](01-state-of-play.md) stack one notch further down, where it stopped too soon, and connects this dossier to a second body of work, on the author's stance toward generative AI (the essays "Showing the Seams" and "Accompanying Individuation").*

This whole document measured sovereignty as a stack, from the lithography machine to the model, asking at each layer two questions: who makes the law, and who can cut off access. But the stack was described one notch short. Below the model layer lies the raw material every model is made of: training data. We did not treat it because it does not look like the other layers. It has no foundry, no oligopoly, no queue. And that is precisely what makes it decisive.

On this layer, a third question appears, one that arose nowhere else in the stack. To the usual two, who makes the law and who can cut off, a third is added: **who consented to being the raw material.** This is the only layer of the entire chain where the question of sovereignty and the question of consent are the same question.

---

## Layer zero: the only upstream Europe can own

Recall the dossier's overall logic: the deeper you go in the stack, the harder the lock, and the more captive Europe is. Data breaks this monotony. It is the lowest layer, beneath the model, and yet it is the only upstream where Europe is not physically barred from ownership.

You cannot etch a leading-edge chip without ASML and without Taiwan. You cannot make HBM without the Asian oligopoly. But you can produce, hold, and qualify data. Europe has it, and of the kind that matters for the next paradigm. The European languages and their corpora, that no one else has any interest in serving. Public, audiovisual, scientific archives. Industrial physical data, German automotive, manufacturing, which we said in [section 05](05-the-bet.md) is the raw material of world models, where the American lead on text does not transfer. The open data of Copernicus. It is an asset that no export control freezes and no foundry conditions.

Put differently, data is to the model layer what non-EUV substrates are to the compute layer: the only upstream terrain where the European advantage can be structural rather than suffered. The dossier's thesis, "own the refining, the tap, and the rule," has a fourth term we had left implicit: **own the source.** Not the crude oil of silicon, which we will not have, but the data table, which we can.

A caveat, so as not to overplay it: owning the source does not exempt you from refining it. Training a model on that data still requires compute, hence GPUs and, upstream, a foundry we have called locked. Data is not a way around the hardware wall; it is the only upstream we can hold in our own right to feed the model layer, where everything else upstream escapes us.

---

## But sovereign data without consent is predation under a European flag

Here the dossier must connect to what it carefully avoided, and which is nonetheless the heart of the public debate: training data has been, everywhere, taken without consent. This is the first of the five grievances that the debate on AI in the arts piles up and conflates: property, labor, personality rights, ecology, cognition. The property grievance says something simple: the models were trained on works without agreement or compensation.

The strategic temptation is then obvious, and the Numerama video states it plainly, as Thierry Breton did in reverse: if Europe wants to compete, it cannot impose on itself agreements its competitors do not impose on themselves; respecting copyright would be a ball and chain while the others scrape everything. So deregulate, to avoid losing the race.

It is a trap, and it must be named with the dossier's own tools. The [fiefdom test](04-lock-in-and-cutoff.md) had a single question: can they cut you off, and would the cutoff be catastrophic. On the data layer, this test has a twin: **did you consent to being the raw material, and can you refuse.** Data taken without agreement is, for whoever produces it, exactly what an API cutoff is for whoever depends on it: a dispossession decided by another, with no accountability. To build a European champion on the non-consented scraping of Europe's citizens, artists, and companies is not to win sovereignty, it is to replace a foreign lord with a local one. Predation does not become sovereign because it changes flags. It is techno-feudalism with an accent.

And strategically, it is shooting oneself in the foot. The only upstream Europe can really own, data, holds its value only from the trust of those who produce it. To liquidate that trust to gain six months on the frontier is to sell off the sovereign asset in order to chase, as a loser, a race we have shown is lost in advance. You do not defend a source by plundering it.

---

## The honesty of the base layer: where consent can lodge, and where it lies

Here a truth both camps avoid must be held, one that forbids the opposite slogan, that of off-the-shelf "ethical" AI. **To date, no general-purpose frontier base model documents a fully consented corpus.** The rare models trained on licensed or public-domain data (Adobe Firefly, for instance) remain confined to specialized uses; the truly capable general-purpose models are scraped. To claim, at the base-model layer, to use an AI with a fully consented corpus is today an alibi, and the critical field is right to mock it.

The consequence is not renunciation, it is precision. Consent is not decreed magically at the unpurifiable layer; it is built where one actually has a hand, and by degrees. At the scale of the state and of industry, this traces a concrete program, which is the regulatory judo of [section 05](05-the-bet.md) applied to data:

- **Genuinely consented data commons.** Today's open corpora show the technical path without crossing it: FineWeb, for instance, is open and documented but remains derived from Common Crawl, hence scraped. The layer to build is another: corpora with consented provenance (public domain, public archives under mandate, negotiated licenses, opt-in contributions), anchored in the European data spaces the EU has already planned, for the verticals no one can exfiltrate: languages, archives, health, industrial data. Not scraped crude, but a cultivated source.
- **The rule as exportable norm, not as handicap.** The GDPR did not kill European industry; it forced part of the global players to align, at least partially, on a European standard (the "Brussels effect"). And the lever already exists, it must be named: the AI Act imposes transparency on training data and respect for copyright, while the DSM directive organizes text and data mining on an opt-out regime. The sovereign bet is therefore not to impose a general opt-in, which would clash with that framework, but to harden transparency and to build, above the legal minimum, corpora with consented provenance as a premium standard: turning an ethical requirement into a market advantage over the 450 million regulated customers.
- **Graduated traceability rather than the binary label.** Not "made by a human" versus "generated by a machine," useless for any real production, but: which data, of what origin, with what agreement, at what submerged cost. It is the infrastructural version, at the scale of a national corpus, of what an author does at the scale of a film by showing the seams. Concretely, this rests on verifiable mechanisms: a provenance registry, an origin-and-license log per dataset, compensation and revocation mechanisms, independent audit. Without them, "consent by design" stays a formula; with them, it is an infrastructure.

The thread is the same as for the rest of the dossier: aim for non-captivity, not purity. We will not purify the inherited base layer. We can decide that the next layer, the one Europe builds, be consented by design, because it is at once the only ethically tenable path and the only strategically durable one.

---

## Verdict

Training data is the layer this dossier had forgotten, and it is the most singular of all. It is the only upstream Europe can own without permission, and it is also the only floor of the stack where sovereignty and consent are inseparable. The two propositions hold together: data sovereignty without consent is merely relocated predation, which destroys the very asset it claims to conquer; consent without an ambition of sovereignty stays a private virtue with no leverage. The bet, here, is neither to scrape like the others nor to dream of a purity that does not exist. It is to make consent the very form of the European advantage on the one upstream resource it can access.

| Vector | Model gap | Compute gap | Foundry gap | European opportunity |
|---------|:---:|:---:|:---:|---|
| Data / consent | **bypasses** | intact | intact | The only ownable upstream, on condition of making it consented |

**In one sentence:** beneath the crude, the refining, the tap, and the rule, there is the source; and the source, Europe will own only by cultivating it with the agreement of those who produce it, never by plundering it at the scale of a continent.
