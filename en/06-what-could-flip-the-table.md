# 06. What Could Flip the Table

[← Index](../README.md) · [← 05 The Bet](05-the-bet.md)

A snapshot lies, as we have said. Everything above describes the present, and the present can shift. This section looks at the forces that could flip the table, holding each to the same discipline so as not to fool ourselves: does it erode the model moat, the compute moat, or the foundry moat? And is it a specifically European opportunity, or simply a change of foreign master?

The overall answer, which we demonstrate vector by vector, is clear. Almost everything can move on the software layer, where Europe can keep pace. Almost nothing moves on the hardware wall, where it is captive. And the only real way out is not a technological vector, it is a change of gaze.

---

## The Wall That Does Not Move

Let us start with what does not change, because it is the floor. None of the vectors explored erodes the foundry, HBM, and materials moat. Several reinforce it: multiplying designs (models, custom accelerators, RISC-V, robots) increases pressure on the same bottleneck, the cutting-edge silicon etched at TSMC with ASML's machines. Silicon cannot be recovered from e-waste. HBM remains an Asian and American oligopoly with no European share whatsoever. And critical materials remain a Chinese weapon. On this wall, pessimism is justified and durable.

## What Really Erodes the Model Layer

This is where pessimism is, by contrast, overrated. The model layer is becoming commoditized before our eyes, and that is good news for Europe.

The **collapse of costs** (the DeepSeek economics: mixture of experts, distillation, quantization, public training recipes) brings the gap with the frontier down to roughly ten points and three to six months, with a cost per token divided by ten every year. The proof that this moat is thin is legal: lacking a technical barrier, the United States now defends it through law. **Small models on edge** deliver tangible usage sovereignty, with no datacenter, immune to cutoff. **Open-weight as a commons**, carried in particular by Hugging Face (a company of French origin that is commoditizing the model layer with two million models and open recipes), is the only floor on which Europe can build a sovereign good without permission. And **decentralized citizen compute**, if it is public, opens an asset that no one can exfiltrate.

All these levers share a limit: they grant usage sovereignty, never material sovereignty. The small model still runs on a chip etched in Taiwan. But usage is already non-captivity, and it is the essence of the fight in the short term.

## What Could Bypass the Wall (Where Europe Holds Real Cards)

Three axes, and they are the only serious hopes for a sovereignty that is not merely a matter of usage.

**Non-EUV substrates** (photonics, in-memory computing, neuromorphic) are manufactured on mature nodes, without EUV. It is the only compute terrain where Europe already owns the chain, and it targets inference, the bulk of tomorrow's volume.

The **next paradigm**, world models, shifts the playing field from text (exhausted, locked) toward industrial physical data (which Europe holds). And the most serious bet there is French, AMI Labs in Paris. If the world tips from text-AI toward physical-AI, the American lead does not transfer mechanically.

**Quantum**, finally, is the field where, unlike generative AI, Europe is genuinely in the race: 54% of the world's processor deployments, a dominance of the hardware bottlenecks (cryogenics, lasers), a unique technological diversity. But two honest caveats. First, quantum does not change the AI picture at five or ten years: it does not replace the GPU, and it is in fact AI that makes quantum work. Second, the real sovereign quantum lever in the short term is not the computer, it is **post-quantum cryptography**: "harvest now, decrypt later," a dated migration, products already certified by ANSSI, a European algorithmic core. It is concrete, urgent, and no one funds it because it is not spectacular.

## The New Fronts That Worsen Dependence

Robotics and space are the two emerging fronts, and both follow the same law: they add dependence, except where a non-negotiable sovereign demand exists.

**Robotics** stacks a materials layer worse than chips: rare-earth magnets are 94% Chinese, and that weapon has already been fired. On the brain (the action models) as on the body (Unitree), Europe is marginal. Except on one point: **defense and drones**, where it has world-class champions (Helsing valued at 18 billion dollars), a captive demand (one does not buy one's war drones from Beijing), a real proving ground (Ukraine), and locked-in public money. War creates the sovereign demand that no civilian policy has managed to create.

**Space** replays the Fable affair exactly: the Starlink precedent, when Musk cut off a critical wartime infrastructure on his own decision, is the foreign switch in orbit. And the perfect symptom is that Europe pays SpaceX to launch its own Galileo satellites. Orbital datacenters are an American chimera. The real European asset is Copernicus open data. And here again, it is defense demand that finally funds sovereignty. The lesson is harsh: sovereignty is not won in orbit, it is lost on the ground, for lack of silicon.

## The Two Variables That Crush Everything

Two forces exceed the technological vectors, and both resist easy optimism.

**Climate and scarcity** can reshuffle the cards in either direction. Either the constraint forces frugality and favors the low-carbon, efficient camp, which would serve Europe. Or, and this is the scenario the comfortable thesis underestimates, it merely confirms the dominance of the best-capitalized (the United States) and the best-endowed in refining (China), relegating Europe to the role of virtuous supplier of clean electrons. Denmark has proven that even the cleanest grid still saturates. Jevons' paradox is a reminder that efficiency multiplies demand instead of reducing it. Frugality is only an advantage if one also has speed and capital.

The **economic AI bubble** is orthogonal to sovereignty, and that is the trap. A crash would destroy American financial capital without transferring any industrial sovereignty to Europe: the foundry wall survives any crash. Worse, it would impoverish the weakest player. The debate over "will the bubble burst" matters less than the question "does Europe have the means to buy when everything is on clearance."

## The Black Swans

The agent tasked with thinking against the grain produced non-obvious scenarios that deserve to be named, because they could change everything.

The **Taiwan lock** that turns ASML into a weapon of reverse embargo: a shock on the island freezes the global foundry, and Europe finds itself, for the span of a narrow window, arbiter of the reconstruction. The **frontier that ceases to matter**: if uses saturate and a capital reversal freezes investment, the six months of American lead lose all value, and European undercapitalization becomes a non-event. The **Europe that mandates interoperability** by law, turning its market of 450 million inhabitants into leverage without owning a single transistor. The **industrial coup**: a golden share that saves the design champions before they leave for the United States. The **forced decoupling**: if Washington blocks access to the frontier, the constraint produces the autonomy that political will has not, just as the Sino-American decoupling produced the Chinese ecosystem in three years. The **public citizen compute** that becomes a sovereign infrastructure for languages and sensitive data. And the **physical AI** that shifts the terrain toward European industrial data, the best window since language models.

---

## The Biggest Blind Spot

At the end of all this analysis, the deepest error is in the measurement itself. We measure sovereignty as a supply chain, and we conclude "vassalage" because Europe manufactures no upstream link. But the capturable and defensible value is not in manufacturing, it is in the control of demand, of integration, and of usage data. An American player that dominates the model, the compute, and the foundry, but must go through European integrators, data, and compliance to reach a regulated market of 450 million people, depends on it.

And there is a second dimension to this blind spot: no analysis seriously models the scenario in which the frontier ceases to have a capturable marginal value. If that day comes, all of Europe's "lag" becomes a non-event, and the indispensability plus fast-follow plus compliance doctrine goes from losing to sufficient.

The table can therefore be flipped. But it flips on the software layer, where Europe can keep pace, and on the axis of the next paradigm and the substrates, where it holds real cards. Not on the hardware wall, which holds. And the most powerful lever is not a technology: it is to stop measuring sovereignty as a factory, and to measure it as a tap and a rule.
