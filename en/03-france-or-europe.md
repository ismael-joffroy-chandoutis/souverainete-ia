# 03. France or Europe: the illusion of national sovereigntism

[← Index](../README.md) · [← 02 Horizons](02-horizons.md)

When a French politician says "sovereignty," you have to ask: at what scale? Because the answer is not the same depending on whether you mean France alone or Europe as a bloc. And this difference is not a matter of vocabulary: it is the line that separates a credible strategy from a slogan.

---

## Europe-as-a-bloc: the only scale where partial sovereignty is credible

At the continental scale, Europe holds one world-class asset, and only one: ASML, the absolute monopoly on EUV lithography machines. It is the single link in the entire chain where the world depends on Europe, and not the other way around. To this is added a real critical mass, both regulatory and financial: 200 billion euros announced, the AI Act, the Cloud and AI Development Act.

But the chain is punctured at every downstream link. No cutting-edge foundry (the most advanced is still the 18nm node at Crolles). Roughly 5% of global compute. No frontier champion. No HBM memory. A near-total dependence on Chinese materials. The result is a partial and defensive sovereignty, not autonomy. This is the fabless trap: having ASML on your soil counts for nothing if Taiwan, where nearly all advanced chips are etched, were to be blocked. Europe-as-a-bloc holds one global card, and controls nothing else.

## France alone: a strategic illusion

France has real but isolated assets. Nuclear energy, its only genuine differentiating advantage (with the caveats we have set out: neither clean, nor fully sovereign, nor immune to climate). Mistral, the national champion, with a defense framework agreement signed on 8 January 2026. The Scaleway cloud, which won the hosting of the Health Data Hub.

But outside the bloc, everything collapses upstream. ASML is Dutch, not French: France alone does not hold the EUV monopoly. Mistral depends on American Nvidia GPUs, etched in Taiwan, on Asian HBM memory, on Chinese rare earths. France alone carries no weight in global compute, when all of Europe is already only at 5%. France maximizes one link, energy, on a chain of which it owns no other tier.

This is where the most widespread political reflex turns out to be the most dangerous illusion. Faced with the Fable affair, the French political class converged, from Bardella ("accelerate Mistral") to Mélenchon ("digital colony"), by way of Attal ("our Strait of Hormuz"), Retailleau ("do it like nuclear") and Philippe ("vassalization"). They are all right about the diagnosis. But "do it like nuclear" is precisely the trap: French civil nuclear power was integrable at the national scale, because the engineering, the grid and the operation were mastered in-house. AI is not. No state of European size can internalize on its own EUV, the foundry, GPUs, HBM and rare earths.

## The Rafale analogy: right about the goal, wrong about feasibility

This is the image that comes up most often, and it deserves to be taken seriously, because it is half true.

It is **right about the goal**. The Rafale embodies operational autonomy: no foreign kill switch, no need for Washington's authorization to take off, unlike an F-35 subject to American constraints. This is exactly what self-hosted open-weight seeks to reproduce: downloadable weights that can be inspected, adjusted and run offline, immune to a remote API shutdown. The Fable affair is precisely the foreign-kill-switch scenario that this doctrine is meant to forbid. On this point, the analogy is exact, and it is the heart of any credible sovereign strategy.

It is **wrong about feasibility**, and for three decisive reasons.

First, the scale of upstream dependence is incomparable. The Rafale is roughly 97% nationally sourceable (Dassault airframe, Safran engine, Thales radar, MBDA missiles). Its residual foreign dependence is marginal and substitutable. The AI chain is the exact opposite: every critical link is outside Europe or outside France.

Next, a weapons system is a stock, AI is a flow. A Rafale already delivered flies even if the factory closes tomorrow; operational autonomy is durable. Frontier AI requires a continuous resupply of compute, of recent GPUs, of electricity, of wafers. A frozen open-weight model stays executable, but its competitiveness at the frontier expires within a few months. Operational sovereignty freezes a dated capability; it does not guarantee staying at the cutting edge.

Finally, the control point is reversed. For the Rafale, France is the holder of the lock: it is the one that can refuse the export. For AI, Europe holds only a single global lock, ASML, and France alone holds none upstream. It stands on the side of the one who is subjected to the switch, not the one who operates it.

---

## Synchronized verdict

France gains sovereignty by strengthening the bloc, not by breaking away from it. National AI sovereigntism is a strategic illusion: a real energy advantage, backed by an entirely foreign upstream chain. Nuclear power and Mistral are not the foundations of a French autonomy; they are contributions to a European sovereignty.

In one sentence: AI sovereignty is a problem of continental scale, of which France holds only a subset of links, Europe a single global lock, and no one the entire chain. The Rafale analogy illuminates the ambition of operational autonomy, but gravely overestimates the possibility of chain autonomy.
