# 05. The bet: indispensability, another axis, demand

[← Index](../README.md) · [← 04 Lock-in and cutoff](04-lock-in-and-cutoff.md)

Before the strategy, a reframing, because it is the reframing that changes everything. The whole analysis that precedes measures sovereignty as a supply chain: who makes the chip, who owns the model, who holds the compute. And measured this way, the conclusion is "vassalage," since Europe controls no decisive upstream link.

But economic history says the opposite. Rent does not lodge itself in the most capital-intensive manufacturing; it migrates toward control of the customer relationship and of usage data. Refined oil captures more value than crude. The operating system more than the hardware. Android more than the phone makers. An American player that dominates the model, the compute and the foundry, but that has to go through European integrators, European data and European compliance to reach 450 million regulated customers, does not have sovereignty over this market: it depends on it.

Mistaking "not making the chip" for "having no power" is the central error of the debate. Sovereignty is not won only at the factory. It is also won at the tap and at the rule.

The realistic bet is therefore neither to catch up with the American stack (lost in advance) nor a purely frugal and decentralized bet (necessary but insufficient, since everything still runs on Nvidia and TSMC). It is a hybrid bet, on three levels.

---

## Level 1: Indispensability: playing the lock we hold

Europe holds only one card of global coercion: ASML. The right use is not blackmail, which would be escalation, but indispensability, which is leverage. The doctrine states itself simply: indispensability, not self-sufficiency. The mechanism is transactional, not martial: guarantee access to European lithography machines in exchange for priority access to foreign chips and industrial counterparts. The goal is not to cut; it is to make any attempt to cut us off too costly to be attempted.

Concretely, this means reclaiming European sovereign control over ASML, through a golden share that is European and not merely Dutch, established before any Taiwan crisis, and reducing the American-origin content in its chain in order to escape the reach of American export controls. Three limits must be faced squarely: Europe does not hold the trigger alone today (ASML operates under Dutch export licensing, already aligned with American pressure); the asymmetry of time makes the weapon useless as direct retaliation (an EUV embargo bites in years, an AI cutoff in hours) but powerful as long-term deterrence; and it is a nuclear option, catastrophic if used. The card is played discreetly, as a reminder that EUV cannot be taken for granted, not as a public threat. Mishandled, we lose our only card.

## Level 2: The other axis: where the European advantage is real

Rather than running, as a loser, after the American frontier, Europe has an interest in opening another axis, where its advantage is structural and not endured.

**Non-EUV substrates** first: photonics, in-memory computing, neuromorphic computing, which are fabricated on mature nodes without EUV, where Europe already holds the chain (GlobalFoundries Dresden, imec, the PIXEurope project, Axelera, the CEA-Leti). This is the only compute terrain where Europe has a specific advantage, and it targets precisely the inference market, which becomes the bulk of the volume once training plateaus.

**The next paradigm** next: world models, where the American lead on text does not transfer. And here the fact is striking: the most serious bet is French. AMI Labs, founded by Yann LeCun, is in Paris, with largely French capital. Wayve, in the United Kingdom, already has a world model at commercial scale. The raw material of this paradigm is no longer the exhausted text corpora but industrial physical data, which Europe possesses (German automotive, manufacturing) and which no American lab can exfiltrate.

**Open-weight as a commons** finally: Mistral, Hugging Face's SmolLM (a company of French origin), OpenEuroLLM, open datasets like FineWeb. This is the layer where Europe can build a common good without anyone's permission. And with it, **small models on edge** (running on a phone or a Raspberry Pi, without a datacenter, immune to the cutoff) and **decentralized citizen compute**, provided it is public and not speculative.

## Level 3: Guaranteed demand and regulatory judo: the lever without a transistor

This is the direct consequence of the reframing. If sovereignty is won at the demand and data layer, then the three most powerful levers require not a single transistor.

First, **sovereign procurement** that is multi-year and guaranteed (defense, health, administration, EuroHPC), so that European champions stop being prey bought up by the Americans. The warning signs are already there: Codasip, a European pillar of RISC-V, is for sale; Ventana has gone to Qualcomm. Without anchor demand, all the European design champions end up siphoned off.

Next, the **interoperability mandate** for models and usage data, on the recipe that has already worked (GDPR, DMA). Forcing the portability of fine-tunes and of data turns closed American models into interchangeable commodities on the market of 450 million, and breaks the cloud lock. Money spent on making migration free undoes more dependence than money spent on building a fiefdom.

Finally, **public citizen compute**, untokenized (on the Flower federated learning brick, of European origin, GDPR-native), for usage sovereignty over the verticals that neither the United States nor China can exfiltrate: health, European languages, archives. Not Bitcoin, a public commons.

And at ground level, where anyone can act without waiting for the state: write anti-lock-in clauses into every critical contract. Escrow or distillation of models, reproducible embeddings, exportable logs, regular switchover tests. Reversibility is not decreed on the day of the cutoff; it is contracted in advance, in cold blood.

The thesis, in one image: Europe cannot own the crude, but it can own the refining, the tap and the rule.

---

## What should be taught

Sovereignty is not only a matter for the state. It is also a hygiene, individual and collective, that can be learned. Here is what deserves to be passed on, without jargon.

**Distinguish three words that get confused.** Autonomy is not depending on others. Sovereignty is making the law of one's own development. Technodiversity is the existence of several technical trajectories, inscribed in several visions of the world. One can be autonomous and a prisoner of a monoculture. The aim is not autonomy within the same world; it is the plurality of worlds.

**Read a technical stack layer by layer.** Lithography, foundry, chip, model, cloud, application. At each level, two questions: who makes its law, and who can cut access? Sovereignty is won or lost layer by layer, never as a block.

**Recognize marketing sovereignty.** A service stamped "sovereign" can remain under foreign legal or material control. The textbook case is Gaia-X: once the American hyperscalers were inside, the initiative lost its purpose. The only criterion that counts: who holds the power to exclude?

**Beware of the gift.** Dependence does not arrive as a constraint; it arrives as a gift: an investment, free access to a model, a win-win partnership. Assess a technological gift by what it then makes impossible.

**Recognize a fiefdom by the cutoff test.** Can they cut you off, and would it be catastrophic? If so, it is not a tool, it is a fiefdom. A tool, you leave it. A fiefdom, it leaves you.

**Own the relationship with your audience.** Your own domain, your own contact list. The platform owns only the audience it lets you reach. A list you can export is the asset hardest to confiscate.

**Prefer what you can leave.** Open formats, exportable data, self-hostable tools, never a single supplier for anything critical. The cost of switching is conceived in cold blood, in advance, not on the day of the cutoff.

**Treat the cutoff as the real risk, not the price.** A downloaded copy, a second rail, a fallback host, before needing them. The lesson of the ICC prosecutor applies to everyone.

**Collective exit beats individual exit.** A fiefdom survives because the captives cannot coordinate to leave. Cooperatives, mutualized infrastructure, regulation that imposes portability: that is where the balance of power tips.

And the common thread, which holds for the state as for the individual: aim for non-captivity, not purity. Zero dependence does not exist; ASML and TSMC are under everything. The goal is not to be pure; it is never to be able to be cut off on something that matters.

---

## The safeguard: why this bet can fail

It has to be said, otherwise the document would be a tract. The frugal and sovereign path is an advantage only if three conditions are met: patient capital, real sovereign procurement, and an unblocked speed of connection to the grid. Without them, it remains a subordinate virtue, a virtuous supplier of clean electrons and efficiency that others cash in.

Worse: a crash of the American AI bubble would not level the playing field. It would destroy American financial capital without transferring a gram of industrial sovereignty to Europe (the foundry wall survives any crash), and it would impoverish the weakest. Europe is already on the edge of recession, and it would be the first to cut its patient capital and its sovereign procurement in case of austerity, while the cash-rich American giants would buy up the fire-sale assets. The real question is not "will the bubble burst," it is "does Europe have a balance sheet solid enough to buy when everything is on sale."

The three-level doctrine must therefore hold whether the bubble bursts or not. That is its condition of credibility.
